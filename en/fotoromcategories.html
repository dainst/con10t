Categories of the DAI Rome's photo archive

Ideal sculpture

Ideal sculpture encompasses stone and bronze sculptures of Greek and Roman art. The subcategories are chosen according to the motifs of the statues and are then organized in alphabetical order. About half of the 25,000 images show mythological creatures or god. Ideal sculptural small bronzes are part of the category „bronze/base metals“, whereas ideals sculptures of portrait head are to be found in the category „portrait sculpture“.
Show ideal sculpture

Portrait sculpture

Portrait sculpture describes Greek, Etruscan-Italic and Roman statues. All portraits are divided into named and unnamed subgroups Greek, Etruscan-Italic, Roman). Further subgrouping is usually done by chronological aspects and within that, the statues are categorized into statues, busts, heads. The largest group is that of Imperial portraits with more than 12,000 sculptures. Portraits on funerary reliefs, sarcophagi and historic reliefs are in part of the respective subcategories.
Show portrait sculpture

Sarcophagi

The overarching term sarcophagi includes Greek, Etruscan, Roman, Jewish, Christian and post-ancient sarcophagi as well as cline monuments. The categories follow the typological criteria, although the main side of the sarcophagus still depicts the main topic. Others parts such as the lid, the sides and fragments are listed separately. Roman sarcophagi are the most with more than 13,000 photographs from which around half are sarcophagi with figural depictions.
Show sarcophagi

Reliefs

Reliefs are grouped into the culture of origin, such as Mycenaean, Greek or Roman. Within these categories, the depictions are categorized into their specific aracheological group (such as funerary reliefs (also urns, votive reliefs, such as vessels, tools or crosses). With more than 10,000 photographs, Roman reliefs are the largest category among the reliefs. Greek reliefs are also well represented with ca. 2,500 photos. Historical reliefs are part of the category „figural architectural decor“.
Show reliefs

Architectural parts

The category of architectural parts is subdivided into the respective parts of the building (f.e. column, base or capital). Within these respective architectural parts, the photographs are grouped into topographic units. Around half of the photographs of this category show capitals from all places of the ancient world and the Middle Ages. Architectural forms that contain many photographs, such as capitals, are further structured according to their orders (Corinthian, Doric, Ionic).
Show architectural parts

Paintings

Paintings follow the structure of topographical cultural circles, f. e. Rome, Greece or Italy. Further, the monuments are grouped according to their place of origin or place of display. Wall paintings from Pompeii are categorized into their Pompeian styles (1st to 4th style) as well as single holdings of museums. The wall paintings from Rome are divided into topographical and chronological units (f. e. 1st-2nd century). Greek paintings also mention the material of the painting (f. e. stelai, sarcophagi, ivory). The majority of depictions of ancient paintings is found among the 4,000 pictures of Pompeian wall paintings.
Shows paintings

Mosaics

One of the largest groups of the photo archive in Rome is that on mosaics with more than 4,000 photographs. Half of these photographs can be found in Italy (f. e. Villa Volusii in Lucus Veroniae, the baptistry of the Arians in Ravenna or the Basilica of Aquileia). The collection organized into the place of display also contains faraway sites in Syria, Algeria or Libya. The Roman photos archive is an important tool for the archaeology of North Africa with more than 500 depictions from Tunisia.
Show mosaics

Topography

In general ancient places, cities or monuments are summarized under the term of topography. It is possible that some overlapping with other categories (reliefs, architectural parts, inscriptions) exists due to detailed images. The topographical objects are divided hierarchically by their countries and cities. Larger cities, such as Rome or Pompeii, are further subdivided into regions. With more than 30,000 photographs, the category topography is the largest in the holding of the Roman photo archive. Most photos are from Italy or North Africa.
Show topography

Vases

The division of vases follows chronological and topographical aspects. The list of Beazley ABV or ARV is the structure followed for the 9,000 depictions of Attic vases. Here, single painters and producers are distinguished. The grouping of Italic vases and their fragments follows A. D. Trendall: Trendall, RVAp; Trendall, LCA; Trendall; RVP. The Attic and Italic vases that are not included in Beazley or Trendall are grouped by their forms and alphabetically by their place of display.
Show vases

Terracotta

The term terracotta includes photographs of artifacts from clay. Vases are excluded due to them having their own category. The objects are structured by their cultural circles (Greek, Roman, Etruscan-Italic) as well as their types (f. e. architecture). The further structure alphabetically follows the places of display of the objects. The main focus is on Etruscan-Italic objects from terracotta (more than 1,000).
Show terracottas

Bronze / base metals

The objects of the group bronze/base metals are organized into their specific forms (mirrors, statuettes, vessels). Large categories such as vessels are also further subdivided (for vessels: dinoi, kraters, amphorai). Hellenistic and Roman bronze statuettes are the focus of the category. Together with bronzes from other times, these make up around 50 % of the photo archive’s holdings. The statuettes are divided into the depicted objects, in order to have the possibility to, for example, only depict images of statuettes of Apollo.
Show bronze / base metals

Minor art

The category minor art contains objects of various materials, mainly glass, gems and precious metals. The subdivision of the group follows the cultural spheres or topographical areas (f. e. Etruscan, Roman or Greek). Around half of the 3,000 photographs show jewelry, such as necklaces, earrings, etc. in gold or silver.
Show minor art

Photo projekt VW-Foundation

The photo projects of the VW-Foundation can be considered a topographical appendix to the systematical department and is made up of the photographical documentation of the project by Sylvia Diebner that was funded by the VW-Foundation from 1981-1984. Stone monuments from ten regions in Italy were recorded who have been published in alphabetical order. Larger inventories of museums are divided into material.
Show photo project VW-Foundation

Italy department

The part of the archive does not contain new photographical material but structures the images of the photo archive according to topographical places in Italy, meaning cities and towns. It contain mainly photographs of figural art in Italian museums and collection in order to give the user another form of access. Thus, the monuments are organized into group of an alphabetically ordered places of origin. If possible, closed material groups or find complexes were left together and recorded in the museums inventory. Larger photographical projects of the institute are ordered after the negative numbers.
