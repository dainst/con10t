With Friedrich Rakob’s bequest, the DAI is indexing one of the most important and most extensive holdings produced within the framework of the DAI Rome’s excavation projects. The materials from more than 35 years of research are housed in the DAI’s photo department in Rome. Work began in 2015 by drawing up a systematic inventory, based on Rakob’s original notes. The archival material consists mainly of photographs – more than 63,000 photographic negatives and slides in total – and around 190 crates and folders containing excavation diaries, drawings, plans and other documents (such as correspondence, preliminary reports and the texts of speeches). It also includes video material (VHS cassettes, discs and CDs, among other media).

All the materials relating to Rakob’s projects are being gradually digitised and made accessible. The main focus of the project to digitise, systematise and index the bequest is on the content of the photographic material and the excavation diaries from Rakob’s projects in Tunisia. The majority of the photographic material and the excavation diaries have already been digitised. An initial selection of the useful photographs was made, and preliminary work undertaken. The indexing of the photographic material began with the black and white negatives that were easily identifiable from a topography folder produced by Rakob himself. These photographs can already be viewed in iDAI.objects/Arachne. Some of the remaining film negatives, slides and a small number of contact prints have been digitised, while work is still ongoing. Each of the datasets stored in the iDAI.objects/Arachne object database is linked with the datasets in the iDAI.gazetteer topography browser. The aim is to make the excavation diaries viewable via iDAI.bookbrowser. According to the archive’s standard, the drawings (find drawings, plans, etc.) are part of the DAI’s so-called ‘Architektenarchiv’ (Architect’s Archive), which has a different digitisation and archiving system than the photographic archive. These holdings will also be indexed in iDAI.objects/Arachne. All the digitised copies and datasets from Rakob’s bequest (including the drawings from the ‘Architektenarchiv’ (Architects’ Archive) shall therefore be brought together in a complete archival collection. Ultimately, the aim is to create a Friedrich Rakob Portal, which will make the materials from Rakob’s bequest publicly accessible and easily usable for further research.

Photographs

The photographic material in Rakob’s bequest includes more than 63,000 negatives, slides and contact prints. The majority of these have already been digitised. The early photographic negatives from the 1950s and 1960s were produced using nitrate films, although a small number of partially large-scale glass plates were also made. Most of these materials were produced during Rakob’s studies. From the excavation campaigns in Chimtou in the early 1960s onwards, the highly combustible nitrate films and fragile glass plates were replaced with standard ‘safety film’. Slides were used to take photographs where colour was useful or effective.

Drawings (plans, sketches and similar)

To date, Rakob’s plans, sketches, drawings and tracings produced between 1953 and 2000 as part of the ‘Architektenarchiv’ (Architects’ Archive) as “Drawings by the Division of Building Archaeology“ of the DAI Rome have been digitised and indexed in an archive. They document the ground plans of buildings and plans of towns or districts; there are also drawings, tracings or sketches of excavation finds and horizontal and vertical strata of the major excavations in Simitthus and Carthage. The bequest also includes further drawings; an accurate estimate of these cannot yet be provided. Although the drawings themselves and their digitised copies belong to the ‘Architektenarchiv’ (Architects’ Archive), they will ultimately be presented as a complete collection together with the other materials.

 

 

Excavation diaries and other documents

The indexing of the written materials in Rakob’s bequest focuses on the excavation diaries (around 14 crates and folders, some containing several diaries). They provide useful information on the excavation works and find contexts, and in some cases are more detailed than the publications. Rakob’s bequest includes his excavation diaries from 1968 to 1984 on Simitthus, Carthage (and Siga) and from 1987 to 1997 on Carthage and Simitthus. The documentation of the excavations also includes preliminary reports (which were not published in this detailed form), letters, inventory lists, invoices (provided they provide insights into how work proceeded, such as exhibitions, personnel, etc.) and other documents on Rakob’s projects, such as the texts of speeches, commented manuscripts and similar documents.
